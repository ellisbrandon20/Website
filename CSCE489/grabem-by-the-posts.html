Bias Over Time

In the these graphs, every news source is given a bias score of 0 to 1. The bias score is calculated by taking the average of every post’s sentiment, where positive is 1, neutral is 0.5, and negative is 0. Therefore, a bias score of 0.5 would indicate neutrality, above 0.5 indicates favorable bias, and below 0.5 indicates unfavorable bias.

Raw Data

After grabbing all 22,000 posts from Facebook, we wanted to analyze it by looking at a few different things. We saw knew that the candidates we covered disproportionately, so we looked into how much each news outlet talked about Trump, Clinton, or Other. In the graph “Number of Posts per News Outlet”, you can clearly see that across all news outlets Trump is the most talked about between the 3 categories. To distinguish what posts fell into each category, we followed the following guidelines. If the post mentions only about Trump, it is a Trump post. If the post mentions only about Clinton, it is a Clinton post. If the post mentions both Trump and Clinton, or mentions anything related to our subject (election, president, etc.) it is classified as an Other post. Lastly, we wanted to observe the distribution of Trump and Clinton posts between each new outlet.

Turk Data

Because of the nature of what was studied, opinions about labels varied significantly. For our labels, we had each post reviewed by 3 turkers and removed any results where all turkers disagreed. As shown in the pie chart, only 39.6% of turkers had a uniform consensus on the bias of a post, while 2 out of the 3 agreed on 53.93% of them. Getting turkers to label our posts with an unbiased opinion is a difficult task, especially when some labeled over 100. We were however able to deny turkers that tried to spam our dataset by removing bogus input (e.g. turkers who labeled everything as positive).

Classifier

Our original classifier began by trying to predict a posts label out of three values: positive, neutral, and negative. But no matter what was done, the accuracy never rose above 55%. In order to combat this, we moved to a classifier that focused on predicting just two different labels: positive and negative. This classifier was able to correctly predict labels with an accuracy of 76.7%, but forced the assumption that every post contained bias. Because of this, we came out with two different classifiers that excelled at telling two different stories. Because the first classifier allowed for neutral posts, it shows a more mild progression of bias with few changes. But with the second classifier, it is much easier to see the variation of bias during small time frames, like when significant events occurred.

When classifying with three labels, more than base accuracy matters. In these pie charts, we display how often the classifier guessed correctly, how often it was off by one (e.g. guessed neutral when post was positive), and how many times it was completely wrong (e.g. guessed negative when post was positive). With this in mind, we build these charts to show the accuracy of our Trump, Clinton, and other classifiers. Note, classification was done with Naive Bayes.

Analysis



Timeline

By observing both timeline charts, “% of Negative Posts about Trump/Clinton”, it's evident the data points are sporadic throughout the first six to seven months. On the latter half of the timeline, it shows that the news outlets are generally posting in the same direction (negative vs positive), but with a slight difference in the degree. This is likely happening because there are multiple candidates for both the Republican and Democratic party at the start of the campaign season, and in the latter half, candidates started to drop out. So in the last few months, the posts become more about Trump or Clinton because of their high probability to win the Republican and Democratic nomination respectively. This inevitably caused a greater number of posts fall in the positive or negative category over time.

These charts point out a time frame to dig deeper into the data set and discover why there was a sudden shift from relatively positive posts to negative posts, and vice versa. For example, in the Clinton timeline, we are able to see that from June to July 2016, she is receiving more positive posts than before. However, from August to October 2016, some event occurred that resulted in the media reporting in a more negative tone. Trump’s ratings improved after the Republican National Convention and received more positive votes than the previous month.

Email

Hillary Clinton’s email scandal was undoubtedly one of the most talked about events of the election, and it shows in the way it was reported by the media. In the wordcloud, you will notice the most common words during this time frame (Sept. 1 - 13) were ‘email’, ‘FBI’, ‘server’, and ‘pneumonia’. While one may assume all news sources would report negatively about Clinton during this time, a completely different story unfolded. Some coverage took a sharp decline, while other news outlets had an increase in positive posts, revealing a potential compromise for the sudden leak of bad news. The only certainty is that the email scandal served to separate the reporting bias of the news outlets instead of uniting them behind a specific case.

Takeaways & Considerations

During this project, we began with Twitter as our original data source, but changed to Facebook when we found out Twitter’s API restrictions only allowed us to grab 3200 tweets per source. For Amazon Turk, we made sure to filter out any spammers, but this task was made more difficult due to bias in the turkers. When building classifiers we originally struggled with getting a good accuracy, but were able to fix this problem when classifying with only two labels.

Things that we would suggest to anyone that tries to recreate this project is to try and use something other than Amazon Turk to get your initial labels for training data. We believe that the turker results may have affected our classifier and its performance in accuracy.
